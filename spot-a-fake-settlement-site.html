How to Spot a Fake Class Action Claim Site

Last checked: manually reviewed · applies to all Canadian settlements

Every real Canadian class action settlement is run by a court-appointed claims administrator — usually a named firm like Deloitte, KPMG, PwC, MNP, Eqitas, or RicePoint. Scam sites copy the language of real notices almost word for word, then swap in a look-alike domain. Here's how to tell them apart before you type in any personal information.

1. It never costs anything to file. Legitimate claims administrators are paid out of the settlement fund itself. Any site asking for a "processing fee," "verification charge," or your credit card number to file a claim is not a real settlement.

2. Check who's asking, not just what they're asking for. Real notices name the specific court that approved the settlement (e.g. the Superior Court of Quebec, the Ontario Superior Court of Justice) and the exact law firm or administrator running the claim process. If a site is vague about who's behind it, that's a warning sign.

3. Search the settlement name plus "class action" plus the administrator's name separately. If a site claims to be run by Eqitas or RicePoint, go find that firm's actual class-action listings directly rather than trusting a link. Real administrators publish the same notice on their own site.

4. Be suspicious of settlements you've never heard reported anywhere else. Real, sizeable Canadian settlements are almost always covered by at least one outlet — Daily Hive, CBC, or local news — before or around the time claims open. If a "settlement" only exists on one obscure site with no independent coverage, treat it as unverified.

5. Look for a real eligibility window, not "everyone qualifies." Legitimate settlements always specify who's covered — a date range, a product, a region. Scam pages tend to claim almost anyone qualifies for a payout, because the goal is to collect as much personal data as possible.

6. Check the claim deadline logic. Real settlements list a specific claim deadline tied to a court-approved timeline, and that date doesn't move if you revisit the site tomorrow. Scam sites sometimes use rolling "urgency" countdowns that reset — a manipulation tactic, not a legal deadline.

Quick rule: if a settlement claim site asks for payment, a full credit card number, or your banking password (rather than just an address for a cheque or e-transfer details after your claim is approved), stop immediately. Real administrators never need your card number to pay you.
